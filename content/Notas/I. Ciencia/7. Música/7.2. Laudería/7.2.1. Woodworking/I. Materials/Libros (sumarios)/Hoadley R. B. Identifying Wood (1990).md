# Introduction

- Identification is based on:
  - main:
    1. **anatomy** = cellular structure;
  - secondary (used sometimes):
    2. **color**;
    3. **odor**;
    4. **density**.

<br>

- Most woods aren't identifiable with an unaided eye (= by tissues = cells seen en masse).
- Some woods are identifiable by their distinct combination of growth rings and rays.
- Identification of most hardwoods:
  1. expose the cell structure with a very sharp cut (e.g., a single-edged razor blade);
  2. examine with a low-power magnifier (e.g., 10×-power hand lens).
- Some woods require examination of individual cells / details of cell walls with a microscope.

<br>

## Example

![alt text](<Imagenes/Tangential surface of an unidentified board, as seen with a hand lens (10x).png>)

- **Rays** — relatively straight, light-colored lines (running vertically through the photo) composed of imperceptibly small ray cells.
  - Most are inconspicuously fine. 2 are bold.
- **Growth rings** — repeating bands of cells perpendicular to the rays.
- **Pores** — cells large enough to be seen individually within growth rings.
  - Exist only in hardwoods.
  - **Ring-porous** wood — the largest pores are concentrated in one portion of a growth ring.
- The rest of the cells are too small to be individually distinct, but their lighter and darker masses are arranged in characteristic patters.

<br>

1. Try identifying by a simple visual match.
2. If a sample is uncertain, use a microscope:
   1. slice off an ultrathin piece of wood with a razor blade;
   2. put it on glass microscope slide in a drop of water and top with a cover glass (see Chapter 7);
   3. examine the cells.
      - E.g., 
        - red oak: the smaller pores are solitary (— don't touch other pores) and thick-walled;
        - white oak: small pores are grouped in ≥2 and thin-walled.

## Microscope

- Identification is easier and more reliable with a microscope than with a hand lens.

# I. The Classification and Naming of Woods

- **Taxonomy** — the practice of classifying living things.

<br>

1. **Kingdom**.
2. **Division** = **phylum**.
3. **Subdivision** = **class**.
4. **Order**.
5. **Family**.
6. **Genus**.
7. **Species** — individual organisms similar in structure and form that can interbreed to produce fertile offspring.

<br>

1. **Spermatophytes** (phylum) — all seed plants.
   1. **Gymnosperms** — exposed seeds.
      1. **Conifers** — softwood.
   2. **Angiosperms** — encapsulated seeds.
      1. **Monocotyledons**.
      2. **Dicotyledons** — hardwood.

## Gymnosperms

![alt text](<Imagenes/Stem forms.png>)

### Coniferales

- **Coniferales** = **conifers**.

<br>

1. **Stem**: excurrent (— a main stem with side branches).
2. **Foliage**:
   1. needle-like / scale-like;
   2. (usually) evergreen.

<br>

- E.g.:
  - pine;
    - bristlecone pine — the oldest plant;
  - fir;
  - spruce;
  - cedar;
  - hemlock;
  - sequoia — the largest plant.

## Angiosperms

- Classified by the amount of initial seed leaves.

### Monocotyledons

- **Monocotyledons** = **monocots** — 1 initial seed leaf.

<br>

- The cell size and arrangement are distinct from those of softwoods and hardwoods.

<br>

- E.g.:
  - temperate climate:
    - grasses;
    - corn;
  - warm climate — can be large and have woody stems in vine / tree form:
    - bamboo;
    - palm;
    - rattan.

### Dicotyledons

- **Dicotyledons** = **dicots** — 2 initial seed leaves.

<br>

1. **Stem**: (typically) dendritic = deliquescent (— a branching main stem).
2. **Foliage**: 
   1. broad-leafed;
   2. (in temperate climate, usually) deciduous (— losing foliage during winter dormancy).

<br>

- E.g.:
  - hardwood;
    - oak;
    - birch;
    - maple;
    - mahogany;
  - string bean;
  - rose.
